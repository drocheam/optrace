5.5. Ray Transfer Matrix Analysis#

5.5.1. ABCD Matrix#

In paraxial optics the relationships between angles \(\theta\) and distances \(x\) relative to the optical axis can be represented in a linear manner. A so-called ABCD-matrix includes the linear components to calculate the output parameters from input values for a system with the same matrix. This can be written as: [1]

(5.89)#\[\begin{split}\left[\begin{array}{l} x_2 \\ \theta_2 \end{array}\right]=\left[\begin{array}{ll} A & B \\ C & D \end{array}\right]\left[\begin{array}{l} x_1 \\ \theta_1 \end{array}\right]\end{split}\]
Table 5.3 Meaning of matrix elements [1]#

Element

Meaning

\(A\)

positional scaling

\(B\)

position change depending on input angle

\(C\)
angle change depending on input position.
Equivalent to \(-\frac{1}{f}\), with \(f\) being the focal distance

\(D\)

angular scaling

Zero matrix elements, as described in Table 5.4, are of particularly importance, giving us cases of imaging and focussing.

Table 5.4 Significance of zero matrix elements [2][3]#

Case

Meaning

\(A=0\)
parallel to point focussing,
output lies at second focal plane

\(B=0\)
point to point focussing (image of an object),
input and output lie at conjugate planes,
\(A\) gives the image magnification

\(C=0\)
parallel to parallel imaging (afocal or telescopic system),
\(D\) gives us the angular magnification

\(D=0\)
point to parallel imaging (e.g. headlamp),
input lies at first focal plane

An important relation is that the determinant of such a ABCD-matrix, here called \(M\), always equals the ratio between ingoing \(n_i\) and outgoing medium \(n_o\). [3]

(5.90)#\[\det (M) = AD - BC = \frac{n_i}{n_o}\]

5.5.2. Propagation through Free Space#

An ABCD-Matrix for free Space with distance \(d\) has the following form: [3]

(5.91)#\[\begin{split}\text{M}_\text{s} = \left[\begin{array}{ll} 1 & d \\ 0 & 1 \end{array}\right]\end{split}\]

5.5.3. Refraction on a Curved Interface#

Refraction on a curved interface with curvature circle \(R\), medium \(n_i\) before and \(n_o\) after the interface leads to the following matrix: [3]

(5.92)#\[\begin{split}\text{M}_\text{c} = \left[\begin{array}{cc} 1 & 0 \\ -\frac{n_o-n_i}{R \cdot n_o} & \frac{n_i}{n_o} \end{array}\right]\end{split}\]

5.5.4. Refraction on a Flat Interface#

With \(R \to \infty\), being equivalent to a flat interface, the matrix becomes: [3]

(5.93)#\[\begin{split}\text{M}_\text{i} = \left[\begin{array}{cc} 1 & 0 \\ 0 & \frac{n_i}{n_o} \end{array}\right]\end{split}\]

5.5.5. Thick Lens#

Thick Lens with refraction index \(n\), front surface curvature \(R_1\), thickness \(d\), back surface curvature \(R_2\), medium with \(n_1\) in front and medium with \(n_2\) behind the lens. Using ray transfer matrix analysis this is the product of the matrix of the front surface matrix \(\text{M}_\text{c1}\), free space propagation matrix \(\text{M}_\text{s}\) and back surface matrix \(\text{M}_\text{c2}\). Note that matrices are multiplied from right to left. The resulting matrix is then: [4]

(5.94)#\[\begin{split}\text{M}_\text{thick} =&~~ \text{M}_\text{c2} \cdot \text{M}_\text{s} \cdot \text{M}_\text{c1}\\ =&~ \left[\begin{array}{cc} 1 & 0 \\ -\frac{n_2-n}{R_2 \cdot n_2} & \frac{n}{n_2} \end{array}\right] \left[\begin{array}{ll} 1 & d \\ 0 & 1 \end{array}\right] \left[\begin{array}{cc} 1 & 0 \\ -\frac{n-n_1}{R_1 \cdot n} & \frac{n_1}{n} \end{array}\right]\\ =&~ \left[\begin{array}{cc} 1 + \frac{n_1-n}{n R_1}d & \frac{n_1}{n}d \\ \frac{n_1 - n}{n_2 R_1} + \frac{n-n_2}{n_2 R_2} + \frac{n_1 - n}{n R_1}\frac{n-n_2}{n_2 R_2}d & \frac{n_1}{n_2} + \frac{n_1}{n}\frac{n - n_2}{n_2 R_2}d \end{array}\right]\end{split}\]

With same ambient media, therefore \(n_0 := n_1 = n_2\), this simplifies to:

(5.95)#\[\begin{split}\text{M}_{\text{thick},n_0} =&~ \left[\begin{array}{cc} 1 + \frac{n_0-n}{n R_1}d & \frac{n_0}{n}d \\ \frac{n_0 - n}{n_0} \left( \frac{1}{R_1} - \frac{1}{R_2} + \frac{n-n_0}{n R_1 R_2}d \right) & 1 + \frac{n - n_0}{n R_2}d \end{array}\right]\end{split}\]

5.5.6. Thin Lens#

Generally element \(C\) can be interpreted as the negative inverse focal length \(-\frac{1}{f}\). Additionally, for a thin lens \(d=0\) holds, simplifying equation (5.94) to:

(5.96)#\[\begin{split}\text{M}_\text{thin} = \left[\begin{array}{cc} 1 & 0 \\ -\frac{1}{f} & \frac{n_i}{n_o} \end{array}\right]\end{split}\]

With \(n_i = n_o\), leading to element \(D=1\), the matrix is consistent with equations found in literature, as [3]

5.5.7. Lensmaker Equation#

As for the thin lens, element \(C\) is equal to \(-\frac{1}{f}\). Negating this element from equation (5.94) and applying \(-(n_1 - n) = (n - n_1)\) gives us the focal length in forward direction:

(5.97)#\[\frac{1}{f_2} = \frac{n-n_1}{n_2}\frac{1}{R_1} - \frac{n-n_2}{n_2}\frac{1}{R_2} + \frac{n-n_1}{n R_1}\frac{n-n_2}{n_2 R_2}d\]

Doing the same calculations with media and curvatures swapped, we get the backward focal length:

(5.98)#\[f_1 = -\frac{n_1}{n_2} f_2\]

Which in written-out form is:

(5.99)#\[\frac{1}{f_1} = -\frac{n-n_1}{n_1}\frac{1}{R_1} + \frac{n-n_2}{n_1}\frac{1}{R_2} - \frac{n-n_1}{n R_1}\frac{n-n_2}{n_1 R_2}d\]

Both equations above are consistent with [3]

For \(n_0 := n_1 = n_2\) we get:

(5.100)#\[\frac{1}{f_2} = \frac{n-n_0}{n_0}\left(\frac{1}{R_1} - \frac{1}{R_2} + \frac{n-n_0}{n R_1 R_2}d \right)\]

Which is the typical form found for the lens maker equation. [5]

5.5.8. Gullstrand Equation#

Utilizing definition (5.110) and equation (5.97) and calling \(D=D_2\) from now on, we can write:

(5.101)#\[D = \frac{n_2}{f_2} = \frac{n-n_1}{R_1} - \frac{n-n_2}{R_2} + \frac{n-n_1}{n R_1}\frac{n-n_2}{R_2}d\]

This is equivalent to

(5.102)#\[D = \frac{n_2}{f_2} = \frac{n-n_1}{R_1} + \left( - \frac{n-n_2}{R_2} \right) - \frac{n-n_1}{R_1} \cdot \left( - \frac{n-n_2}{R_2} \right) \frac{d}{n}\]

With the surface optical powers \(D_\text{s1} = \frac{n-n_1}{R_1}\) and \(D_\text{s2} = -\frac{n-n_2}{R_2}\) this simplifies to:

(5.103)#\[D = D_\text{s1} + D_\text{s2} - D_\text{s1} D_\text{s2} \frac{d}{n}\]

Which is known as Gullstrand’s equation. [6][7]

5.5.9. Cardinal Points#

The following calculations are derived from [8] and [3]. Also there you can find a textual and graphical explanation of cardinal points and planes.

Vertex Points

The vertex points \(V_1\) and \(V_2\) are the position of the lens front and center at the optical axis.

Principal Points

(5.104)#\[\begin{split}P_1 =&~ V_1 - \frac{n_1 - n_2 D}{n_2 C}\\ P_2 =&~ V_2 + \frac{1-A}{C}\end{split}\]

Nodal Points

(5.105)#\[\begin{split}N_1 =&~ V_1 - \frac{1-D}{C}\\ N_2 =&~ V_2 + \frac{n_1 - n_2 A}{n_2 C}\end{split}\]

Focal Lengths

Focal lengths are given by the negative inverse of \(C\) as well as equation (5.98).

(5.106)#\[\begin{split}f_1 =&~ \frac{n_1}{n_2 C}\\ f_2 =&~ -\frac{1}{C}\end{split}\]

Focal Points

Both focal points are there corresponding focal lengths away from their principle plane.

(5.107)#\[\begin{split}F_1 = &~ P_1 + f_1\\ F_2 = &~ P_2 + f_2\end{split}\]

EFL, BFL, FFL

Effective focal length (EFL), back focal length (BFL) and front focal length (FFL) are defined as follows:

(5.108)#\[\begin{split}\text{FFL} =&~ F_1 - V_1 &=~& &\frac{D}{C}\\ \text{BFL} =&~ F_2 - V_2 &=~& -&\frac{A}{C}\\ \text{EFL} =&~ f_2 &=~& -&\frac{1}{C}\end{split}\]

5.5.10. Optical Power#

The default definition in optrace is the optical power being the inverse of the geometric focal length.

(5.109)#\[\begin{split}D_1 = \frac{1}{f_1}\\ D_2 = \frac{1}{f_2}\end{split}\]

The alternative definition below has the advantage, that \(D_\text{1n} = -D_\text{2n}\) holds, independent of the refractive media. However, focal lengths are not the actual distance between principal plane and focal points.

For \(n_1 = n_2 = 1\) both definitions are equivalent.

(5.110)#\[\begin{split}D_\text{1n} =&~ \frac{n_1}{f_1}\\ D_\text{2n} =&~ \frac{n_2}{f_2}\\ f_\text{1n} =&~ \frac{f_1}{n_1}\\ f_\text{2n} =&~ \frac{f_2}{n_2}\\ \text{EFL}_n =&~ \frac{f_2}{n_2}\\\end{split}\]

5.5.11. Lens Setups#

To evaluate setups of \(N\) lenses the lens matrices \(\text{M}_\text{L,i}\) and the free space matrices \(\text{M}_\text{s,j}\) with \(i \in \{0, 1, \dots, N\},~~ j \in \{0, 1, \dots, N-1\}\) need to be multiplied.

(5.111)#\[\text{M} = \text{M}_\text{L,N} \cdot \text{M}_\text{s,N-1} \dots \text{M}_\text{s,0} \cdot \text{M}_\text{L,0}\]

5.5.12. Optical Center#

General Case

From the yellow triangle follows that:

(5.112)#\[\tan \phi = \frac{x_2 - x_1}{V_2 - V_1}\]

While from the green triangle follows that:

(5.113)#\[\tan \phi = -\frac{x_1}{o}\]

Note that the minus wass added so both equations have the same sign. Inserting (5.112) into (5.113) gives us:

(5.114)#\[o = - \frac{x_1}{x_2 - x_1} \left(V_2 - V_1\right)\]

The blue triangle leads to:

(5.115)#\[\tan \theta_1 = -\frac{x_1}{N_1 - V_1}\]

With paraxial rays \(\theta_1 \approx \tan \theta_1\) holds. \(x_2\) can therefore be calculated using the ABCD matrix of the setup:

(5.116)#\[x_2 = A x_1 - \frac{B}{N_1 - V_1} x_1\]

Inserting into (5.114) gives us:

(5.117)#\[o = -\frac{x_1}{A x_1 - \frac{B}{N_1 - V_1} x_1 -x_1} \left(V_2 - V_1\right)\]

From (5.105) follows that:

(5.118)#\[N_1 - V_1 = - \frac{1 - D}{C}\]

Which can also be inserted into the equation (5.117). After some rearranging one gets:

(5.119)#\[o = \frac{V_2 - V_1}{1 - A + \frac{BC}{D-1}}\]

Which needs to be added to the front vertex to get the absolute position of the optical center:

(5.120)#\[\text{OC} = V_1 + \frac{V_2 - V_1}{1 - A + \frac{BC}{D-1}}\]

The requirements that were silently assumed are that the nodal point exist (\(C \neq 0\)) and input and output positions differ \(x_2 \neq x_1\). The only case where it makes sense to also define an optical center is for an ideal lens, where we set \(\text{OC} = V_1\), although a nodal ray does not cross the optical axis at that point. For all other cases, especially \(1 - A + \frac{BC}{D - 1} = 0\) or \(D = 1\) the optical center is undefined. As mentioned before, an ideal lens (\(A = 1, ~B=0, ~C\neq 0, ~D=1\)) is an exception.

Thick Lens/Lens Combination with Same Front and Back Medium

With \(m := \frac{n - n_0}{n}d\) matrix (5.95) becomes:

(5.121)#\[\begin{split}\text{M}_{\text{thick},n_0} =&~ \left[\begin{array}{cc} 1 - \frac{m}{R_1} & \frac{n_0}{n}d \\ \frac{n_0 - n}{n_0} \left( \frac{1}{R_1} - \frac{1}{R_2} + \frac{m}{R_1 R_2} \right) & 1 + \frac{m}{R_2} \end{array}\right]\end{split}\]

The denominator of equation (5.120) is then:

(5.122)#\[\begin{split}1 - A + \frac{BC}{D - 1} &= 1 - \left(1 - \frac{m}{R_1}\right) + \frac{\frac{n_0-n}{n_0}\frac{n_0}{n}d \left( \frac{1}{R_1} - \frac{1}{R_2} + \frac{m}{R_1 R_2} \right)}{1 + \frac{m}{R_2} - 1}\\ &= \frac{m}{R_1} - m \frac{\left( \frac{1}{R_1} - \frac{1}{R_2} + \frac{m}{R_1 R_2} \right)}{\frac{m}{R_2}}\\ &= \frac{m}{R_1} - \left( \frac{R_2}{R_1} - 1 + \frac{m}{R_1} \right)\\ &= 1 - \frac{R_2}{R_1}\end{split}\]

Leading to the final form of (5.120):

(5.123)#\[\text{OC} = V_1 + \frac{V_2 - V_1}{1 - \frac{R_2}{R_1}}\]

Equation (5.123) is consistent with the results in [9]. As mentioned in [10] for this case the optical center is completely independent of the wavelength and the material dispersion.

In fact, doing this with two ideal lenses with focal lengths \(f_1\) and \(f_2\) with separation distance \(d\) and the same ambient media, produces a similar form:

(5.124)#\[\begin{split}M_\text{2L} &= \left[\begin{array}{cc} 1 & 0 \\ \frac{1}{f_2} & 1 \end{array}\right] \cdot\left[\begin{array}{ll} 1 & d \\ 0 & 1 \end{array}\right] \cdot\left[\begin{array}{cc} 1 & 0 \\ \frac{1}{f_1} & 1 \end{array}\right]\\ &= \left[\begin{array}{cc} 1+\frac{d}{f_1} & d \\ \frac{1}{f_1}+\frac{1}{f_2}+\frac{d}{f_1 f_2} & 1+\frac{d}{f_2} \end{array}\right]\end{split}\]
(5.125)#\[\begin{split}1 - A + \frac{BC}{D - 1} &= 1 - \left(1 + \frac{d}{f_1}\right) + \frac{ \frac{1}{f_1} + \frac{1}{f_2} + \frac{d}{f_1 f_2} }{1 + \frac{d}{f_2} - 1} d\\ &= -\frac{d}{f_1} + \frac{f_2}{f_1} + 1 + \frac{d}{f_1}\\ &= 1 + \frac{f_2}{f_1}\end{split}\]
(5.126)#\[\text{OC} = V_1 + \frac{V_2 - V_1}{1 + \frac{f_2}{f_1}}\]

For \(R_2 = -R_1\) in (5.123) or \(f_2 = f_1\) in (5.126) the optical center lies at exactly the center of the lens/lens combination.

5.5.13. Image and Object Distances#

Positions

The matrix for additional object distance \(g\) and image distance \(b\) is:

(5.127)#\[\begin{split}\text{M}_\text{b,g} = \left[\begin{array}{ll} 1 & b \\ 0 & 1 \end{array}\right] \cdot \text{M} \cdot \left[\begin{array}{ll} 1 & g \\ 0 & 1 \end{array}\right]\end{split}\]

Where distance \(b\) is given relative to the lens vertex point \(V_2\) and \(g\) relative to \(V_1\) and both distances being positive when pointing towards positive z-direction.

For imaging element \(B_\text{b,g} = \text{M}_\text{b,g}[0, 1]\) needs to be zero. This means the output ray position \(x_2\) is independent on the input angle \(\theta_1\) and only dependent on the input position \(x_1\).

This gives us:

(5.128)#\[B_\text{b,g} = g (A + C b) + B + D b = 0\]

For \(b, g \in \mathbb{R}\)

(5.129)#\[\begin{split}b(g) = \begin{cases} -\frac{B + g A} {D + g C}, &~ \text{for}~~ {D + g C} \neq 0\\ \mathbb{R} &~ \text{for}~~ {D + g C} = 0 ~~\text{and}~~ B + g A = 0\\ \emptyset &~ \text{for}~~ {D + g C} = 0 ~~\text{and}~~ B + g A \neq 0 \end{cases}\end{split}\]
(5.130)#\[\begin{split}g(b) = \begin{cases} -\frac{B + b D} {A + b C}, &~ \text{for}~~ {A + b C} \neq 0\\ \mathbb{R} &~ \text{for}~~ {A + b C} = 0 ~~\text{and}~~ B + b D = 0\\ \emptyset &~ \text{for}~~ {A + b C} = 0 ~~\text{and}~~ B + b D \neq 0 \end{cases}\end{split}\]

For special cases around \(\pm\infty\) we get:

(5.131)#\[\begin{split}\lim_{g \to \pm \infty} b(g) = \begin{cases} -\frac{A}{C} &~ \text{for}~~ {C} \neq 0\\ \mathbb{R} &~ \text{for}~~ C = 0 ~~\text{and}~~ A = 0\\ \emptyset &~ \text{for}~~ C = 0 ~~\text{and}~~ A \neq 0 \end{cases}\end{split}\]
(5.132)#\[\begin{split}\lim_{b \to \pm \infty} g(b) = \begin{cases} -\frac{D}{C} &~ \text{for}~~ {C} \neq 0\\ \mathbb{R} &~ \text{for}~~ C = 0 ~~\text{and}~~ D = 0\\ \emptyset &~ \text{for}~~ C = 0 ~~\text{and}~~ D \neq 0 \end{cases}\end{split}\]

Optrace sets all cases with \(\emptyset, \mathbb{R}\) to NaN (not a number), since both are impractical.

For \(\text{M} = \text{M}_\text{thin}\) (thin lens approximation) from equation (5.96) the equations simplify to

(5.133)#\[b(g) = \frac{fg}{g-\frac{n_i}{n_o}f}\]
(5.134)#\[g(b) = \frac{\frac{n_i}{n_o}fb}{b-f}\]

Which is the form known from the imaging equation:

(5.135)#\[\frac{n_o}{f} = \frac{n_i}{g} + \frac{n_o}{b}\]

And most commonly found for \(n_i = 1, ~~n_o = 1\): [11]

(5.136)#\[\frac{1}{f} = \frac{1}{g} + \frac{1}{b}\]

Where \(f,~b,~g\) are positive when measured in positive z-direction.

Magnifications

With a given object and image position the combined ABCD matrix in (5.127) can be calculated. Element \(A\) of the matrix corresponds to the magnification factor \(m\), as \(B=0\) applies for this matrix.

For a thin, ideal lens \(m\) would be equal to \(b/g\).

Referring to the value of this factor, \(A < 0\) signifies an inverted image, \(A > 0\) an upright image, \(\lvert A \rvert > 1\) a size increase, \(\lvert A \rvert < 1\) a size decrease.

5.5.14. Entrance and Exit Pupils#

To calculate the entrance and exit pupil for a given system and aperture stop the lens setup is separated into a front and rear group. The pupils are then the image of the aperture for the corresponding group. [12]

Aperture inside setup

In the case of the aperture stop being inside the lens setup, the setup and its matrix \(\text{M}\) can be separated into three parts:

(5.137)#\[\text{M} = \text{M}_\text{rear} \cdot \text{M}_\text{gap} \cdot \text{M}_\text{front}\]

\(\text{M}_\text{front}\) is the matrix for all surfaces in front of the aperture stop, \(\text{M}_\text{back}\) for everything behind the stop. \(\text{M}_\text{gap}\) is the distance matrix for the gap region between surfaces where the stop is positioned in and does not belong to either front or back group.

The entrance pupil is the result of the stop being imaged into the front group, whereas the exit pupil is the image of the aperture stop into the rear group.

The object distance for the exit pupil is:

(5.138)#\[g_\text{ex} = V_{1,\text{rear}} - z_\text{s}\]

The exit pupil image distance \(b_\text{ex}\) is calculated with \(\text{M}_{b,g} = \text{M}_\text{rear}\) and the procedure in Section 5.5.13. The resulting \(z_\text{ex}\) is then:

(5.139)#\[z_\text{ex} = V_{2,\text{rear}} + b_\text{ex}\]

For the entrance pupil, we need to image the stop in backwards direction through \(\text{M}_\text{front}\). This is done by inverting the matrix:

(5.140)#\[\begin{split}\text{M}^{-1}_\text{front} &= \left[\begin{array}{cc} A & B \\ C & D \end{array}\right]^{-1}\\ &= \frac{1}{AD - BC} \left[\begin{array}{cc} D & -B \\ -C & A \end{array}\right]\\\end{split}\]

The object distance is negative and calculated with the back vertex of the front group:

(5.141)#\[g_\text{en} = V_{2,\text{front}} - z_\text{s}\]

Calculating \(b_\text{en}(g_\text{en})\) is done with the help of the procedure in Section 5.5.13 and by setting \(\text{M}_{b,g} = \text{M}^{-1}_\text{front}\). The image distance \(b_\text{en}\) then needs to be added to the front vertex of the front group to get the entrance pupil position:

(5.142)#\[z_\text{en} = V_{1,\text{front}} + b_\text{en}\]

Aperture in front of setup

With the stop being in front of all lenses, it itself is the entrance pupil (\(z_\text{en} = z_\text{s}\)).

The exit pupil then is the result of imaging through all elements with the matrix:

(5.143)#\[\text{M}_\text{rear} = \text{M}\]

Aperture behind of setup

With the stop being behind all lenses, it itself is equal to the exit pupil (\(z_\text{ex} = z_\text{s}\)).

The entrance pupil then is the result of imaging backwards through all elements with the procedure above and the matrix:

(5.144)#\[\text{M}_\text{front} = \text{M}\]
